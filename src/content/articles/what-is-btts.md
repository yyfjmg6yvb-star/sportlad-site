## What does BTTS mean?

BTTS stands for **Both Teams to Score**. It's a straight yes/no bet: does at least one goal go in for each side during the 90 minutes (plus stoppage time — extra time and penalties in cup competitions don't count unless the market says otherwise)?

You don't need to predict the scoreline, who wins, or how many goals — just whether both teams get on the scoresheet at some point.

## Why is it popular?

Two reasons. It's simple to understand compared to something like correct score, and it turns matches that look dull on paper into something worth watching — a 0-0 bore draw becomes tense the moment either side is one goal away from settling it.

## How do the odds actually work?

Bookmakers price BTTS based on both teams' attacking and defensive form, not just the final result. Two mid-table sides who both leak goals but also score freely can have short BTTS-Yes odds even if neither is expected to win. Meanwhile a heavy favourite with a rock-solid defence facing a poor away side can have surprisingly long BTTS-Yes odds, because the market expects a clean sheet.

That's the first mistake beginners make: assuming the favourite scoring means BTTS is likely. It only helps if the *other* team scores too.

## When does BTTS actually pay off?

Look for:

- **Two attack-minded sides with leaky defences** — the classic BTTS match, regardless of the final result
- **Games with something to prove at both ends** — a struggling team at home often still finds a goal even in a loss
- **Avoid it when one side has a genuinely strong defensive record and nothing to gain from an open game** — cup ties late in a competition, relegation six-pointers, and "must not lose" fixtures often see BTTS-No come in instead

## A word on combining it with other markets

BTTS is often paired with over/under goals or the match result in an accumulator. Worth knowing: stacking BTTS-Yes with Over 2.5 Goals isn't "the same bet twice" — you can have both teams score in a 1-1 draw (BTTS-Yes, Under 2.5) or a 4-0 win (BTTS-No, Over 2.5). They're related but genuinely separate outcomes, and treating them as interchangeable is a common way accumulators quietly get more expensive than punters realise.
